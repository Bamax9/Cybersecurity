Chapter 1 · Hacks

How Hacks Work

A "hack" is when someone tries to get into a computer or account without permission. Tap each type below to learn how it works.

Phishing

Phishing (sounds like "fishing") is when a bad person sends a fake message that looks real — trying to trick you into giving away your password.

⚠
Example: You get an email saying "Your account will be deleted! Click here now to save it!" — but the link goes to a fake website that steals your password.
🔎
Check the sender's email address carefully. Fake ones look almost right but have a small typo — like support@g00gle.com instead of google.com
🚨
Phishing messages always use scary, urgent words like "Act NOW!" or "Your account is in DANGER!" Real companies do not panic you like that.
✓
Safety rule: When in doubt, never click the link. Go directly to the website yourself by typing the address in your browser.
1 of 4

Password Attacks

Hackers use programs that guess thousands of passwords very fast. If your password is simple, it gets cracked in seconds!

⚠
Weak passwords that get guessed instantly: "password", "123456", your name, your birthday.
🔑
A strong password is long and mixes capital letters, numbers, and symbols. Example: T1ger$JumpH1gh! — much harder to crack!
💡
Never use the same password on two different websites. If one gets hacked, the bad person could get into your other accounts too.
✓
Safety rule: Ask a trusted adult about using a password manager — it remembers all your strong passwords for you!
2 of 4

Social Engineering

This type of hack does not use computers — it tricks people instead! The hacker pretends to be someone trustworthy to get your secret info.

💬
Example: Someone calls and says "Hi, I'm from your internet company. I need your password to fix a problem." — Real companies NEVER ask for your password this way.
🎭
Hackers might pretend to be: a tech helper, a game company, a teacher, or even a friend. They want you to feel comfortable so you share private info.
⚡
They often create urgency to stop you from thinking carefully: "You only have 5 minutes before your account is gone forever!"
✓
Safety rule: No real company or helper will ever ask for your password. If someone does — it is always a trick. Tell a trusted adult immediately.
3 of 4

Man-in-the-Middle Attack

Imagine passing a note to a friend, but someone in the middle secretly reads it. This attack does the same — but with your internet messages!

📡
This can happen on public WiFi networks — like at a restaurant or library — where the connection might not be properly secured.
🔒
Look for the padlock icon in your browser's address bar. That means the website is encrypted — your data is turned into a secret code that bad people cannot read.
⚠
Be extra careful about typing passwords on public WiFi. If you see a warning that the website is not safe — leave immediately!
✓
Safety rule: Always look for the padlock before entering any passwords. If it is missing, do not type anything private.

Chapter 2 · Viruses

How Computer Viruses Work

Just like a cold virus spreads from person to person, a computer virus spreads from computer to computer. Tap each step to learn more.

What is a computer virus?

A computer virus is a tiny, sneaky program made by a bad person. Its job is to copy itself and spread — just like a cold spreading between people.

Once it gets into your computer, here is what it can do:

  • ✕
    Delete or scramble your files and photos
  • ✕
    Make your computer run extremely slow
  • ✕
    Secretly spy on what you type, like passwords
  • ✕
    Spread itself to other computers through emails or USB drives
  • ✕
    Show you lots of unwanted advertisements
💡
Viruses are completely invisible to the eye. You cannot see them — but antivirus software can detect and remove them!
1 of 4

How does a virus get onto your computer?

Viruses hide inside things that look safe or interesting. Here are the most common ways they sneak in.

📧
Email attachments: A file in an email — like a picture or game — can secretly contain a virus. Never open attachments from people you do not know!
🌐
Bad websites: Some websites automatically try to download viruses just by you visiting them. Stick to websites you know and trust.
💾
USB drives: Plugging in someone else's USB drive can transfer a virus. Always ask an adult before plugging anything in.
🎮
Fake free downloads: "Get this game for free!" from a strange website might have a virus hiding inside the download.
2 of 4

Different kinds of malware

"Malware" means any bad program. There are several different kinds, each with a different trick.

  • 🦠
    Virus — copies itself and spreads to other files and computers
  • 🐛
    Worm — travels through networks all by itself without any help from you
  • 🐴
    Trojan — hides inside something helpful (like a free app) but does harmful things
  • 🕵
    Spyware — secretly watches everything you do and sends it to the bad person
  • 💰
    Ransomware — locks all your files and demands money to unlock them
💬
Ransomware is especially dangerous. It is always a good idea to have backups of important files — ask a parent about this!
3 of 4

How to stay protected

The good news: staying safe from viruses is simple if you follow these rules every day!

  • ✓
    Use antivirus software — it works like a security guard for your computer
  • ✓
    Keep your computer and apps updated — updates often fix security problems
  • ✓
    Do not download things from websites you do not trust
  • ✓
    Do not click on pop-up ads that say "You won a prize!"
  • ✓
    Ask an adult before installing any new program
  • ✓
    Keep copies of important files backed up somewhere safe

Chapter 3 · Scareware

What Is Scareware?

Scareware uses fear to control you. It wants you to panic so you do something you should not. Let's break it down step by step.

How scareware works

Scareware is a fake warning that pops up on your screen, designed to make you feel scared so you click something or buy something without thinking carefully.

⚠
A big scary red pop-up appears: "YOUR COMPUTER HAS 47 VIRUSES! CLICK HERE NOW OR YOUR FILES WILL BE DESTROYED!" — This is completely fake.
🎭
If you click it, one of two things happens: it either tries to install a real virus, or it asks you to pay money for a fake "security program" that does nothing at all.
💡
Important: Your computer is almost certainly fine. The pop-up has no idea what is on your computer — it is just guessing to scare you into clicking.
1 of 5

Warning signs to look for

Once you know the pattern of scareware, you will recognize it immediately. Every scareware message has these signs.

  • !
    It appears suddenly as a pop-up — you did not ask for it
  • !
    It uses very scary, urgent language like "IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED"
  • !
    It shows a dramatic number of "viruses found" like 47 or 112
  • !
    It flashes bright colors, plays alarm sounds, or spins
  • !
    It asks you to call a phone number right away
  • !
    It tries to stop you from closing it or going back
✓
Real antivirus programs are calm and quiet. They do not flash, scream, or try to rush you. They are already installed — they do not pop up from random websites!
2 of 5

Different types of scareware

Scareware comes in several forms, but they all use the same trick — fear.

💻
Fake virus scanners — pretend to scan your computer and "find" fake viruses to frighten you into buying something
📞
Tech support scams — show a fake warning and a phone number; a real person then tries to trick you on the phone
🔒
Fake lock screens — pretend your screen is locked and claim you broke a law, demanding that you pay money to unlock it
🌐
Browser pop-up scams — appear inside your web browser, styled to look like an official Windows or Mac warning message
3 of 5

Exploitation scareware

Some scareware goes beyond just scaring you — it uses your fear to actually cause real harm by tricking you into giving something up.

🪤
Fake payments: They show a scary pop-up and ask you to pay with a gift card or credit card to "fix" your computer. Real tech companies never ask for payment this way.
🖥️
Remote access tricks: They ask you to install a program that lets them control your computer from far away — then they can steal files and passwords.
🪪
Identity theft: They use the fake emergency to get you to type in your name, address, or passwords — which they then keep and misuse.
⚠
Once a bad person has your personal information or remote access to your computer, the damage can be very serious. This is why it is so important to never click or give anything before asking a trusted adult!
4 of 5

What to do if you see scareware

Stay calm — you have a plan. Follow these steps and you will be completely fine!

  • ✓
    Stay calm — it is almost certainly fake and your computer is fine
  • ✓
    Do NOT click anything inside the pop-up window
  • ✓
    Close the browser tab using the X button at the top of the screen
  • ✓
    If it will not close, ask an adult to help close it safely
  • ✓
    Never call any phone number shown in a scary pop-up
  • ✓
    Never download anything because of a scary message
  • ✓
    Tell a trusted adult what happened right away
⭐
Golden rule: The more something tries to scare you, the more suspicious you should be. Real safety tools never panic you!

Chapter 4 · Exploitation

Exploiting Legitimate Tools

Sometimes bad people do not use viruses or fake websites. Instead, they hijack normal, everyday tools — things that are supposed to be helpful — and use them to trick you.

What does "exploiting legitimate tools" mean?

A legitimate tool is something real and normal — like browser notifications, calendar invites, or search results. Exploitation means a bad person twists that normal tool into something harmful.

🔧
Why do bad people do this? Because normal tools are already trusted by your computer and browser — so they can bypass security checks that would normally block suspicious programs.
🎭
It is like a stranger sneaking into a party by wearing a staff uniform. The uniform is real, but the person wearing it has bad intentions.
💡
This makes exploitation especially tricky — because the tool doing the harm is something your device already trusts. That is why learning to recognize it is so important!
1 of 4

Browser notifications

Browser notifications are a real feature — websites can ask permission to send you pop-up alerts. But bad websites trick you into saying yes, then spam you with fake warnings.

⚠
How the trick works: A website shows a pop-up that says "Click Allow to prove you are not a robot" or "Click Allow to watch the video." Once you click Allow, they can send scary fake alerts to your screen any time — even when you are not on that website!
🔔
These fake notifications often look exactly like real system alerts. They might say "VIRUS DETECTED" or "Your computer is at risk!" — but they are just browser notifications, not real warnings.
👀
Real websites that need notifications — like news sites or school tools — will show a small browser bar at the top asking permission. They will not block you from the page or trick you into clicking.
✓
Safety rule: Almost always click "Block" or "Don't Allow" when a website asks to send you notifications — especially if you do not know the website well. If you accidentally allowed it, ask an adult to help you remove it in browser settings.
2 of 4

Other normal tools that get abused

Browser notifications are just one example. Here are other everyday tools that bad people sometimes hijack.

🔍
Search engine ads: When you search for something, the first results are sometimes paid ads. Bad people pay to put fake websites at the top of search results so they look official.
📅
Calendar invites: Spam invites get added to your calendar automatically, with links that say "You have won a prize — click here to claim it!" The calendar feature is real, but the invite is fake.
📲
App permissions: A game or app asks for permission to access your camera, microphone, or contacts — far more than it actually needs. The app is real, but it is collecting information it should not have.
📧
Email auto-fill: Your browser remembers email addresses to help you type faster. Some bad websites are designed to secretly grab that saved info without you noticing.
3 of 4

How to protect yourself

Once you know how these tricks work, you have the power to stop them. Here is what to do.

  • ✓
    Always click "Block" or "Don't Allow" when unknown websites ask to send notifications
  • ✓
    Scroll past the top search results — real official websites are usually not the first ads you see
  • ✓
    Delete calendar invites from people you do not know without clicking any links inside them
  • ✓
    When installing an app, check what permissions it is asking for — a game does not need your contacts!
  • ✓
    Ask an adult to review your browser notification settings and remove any websites you do not recognize
  • ✓
    If a tool or feature is behaving strangely, tell a trusted adult before clicking anything
⭐
Remember: The tool being used is real — that is what makes this tricky. Your superpower is pausing and asking "Did I actually ask for this?" before trusting it.
